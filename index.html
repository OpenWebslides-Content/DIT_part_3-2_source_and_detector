X-ray sources

X-ray sources

  • Different mechanisms can lead to the production of X-rays. (SEM)
  • When charged particles hit a target material, they will release their kinetic energy in the form of heat and electromagnetic radiation (so called Brehmstralung). This principle can be used to generate X-rays by accelerating electrons so they hit a target anode.
  • In 1913: the invention of the Coolidge tube => an important milestone in X-ray radiography.

X-ray sources

The Coolidge tube

In this design, in glass tube a high vacuum and electrons are emitted from a heated filament toward a positively charged anode

Backside of anode can be cooled (necessary since most of the electron energy is converted into heat)

The strength of the Coolidge tube is its’ tunability:

  • energy of X-rays can be easily adjusted by changing the accelerating voltage
  • filament current can be changed to produce the desired electron beam current

Until today, the majority of X-ray tubes are still based on this design.

Different length scales for geomaterials

medical and industrial CT:

  • setup rotates around object (patient)
  • up to 250 µm
  • high X-ray flux → short scanning time
  • sensitive detector elements (high contrast and signal-to-noise-ratio)
  • large samples possible

Different length scales for geomaterials

micro-CT:

  • fixed source and detecter, rotating sample
  • small X-ray spot
  • resolution up to 1 µm
  • lower X-ray flux → longer scanning time
  • small sample

Different length scales for geomaterials

nano-CT:

  • spot size below 1 µm (“nano-focus tubes”)
  • resolution up to 400 nm → ‘sub-micron-focus tubes’
  • typical sample size: 1 mm

Synchrotron vs lab

Typical acquisition times for a full scan is 5–10 min for synchrotron sources

Typical acquisition times for a full scan goes from 15 min to 24 h for lab-based systems depending on resolution, energy level, and the chosen exposure time (which affects noise in the images)

X-ray DETECTORS

X-ray detectors

Many types of detectors are suitable for X-rays.

X-rays can be detected in a direct or indirect manner.

Direct detector:

  • incoming X-rays interact in the sensitive area of the detector
  • possibility to count single photons, and derive their energy => has significant advantages in imaging applications, yet needs further development. (Medipix-Pilatus)

X-ray detectors

Many types of detectors are suitable for X-rays.

X-rays can be detected in a direct or indirect manner.

Indirect detector:

  • X-ray photons are first converted to visible light by a scintillator => This visible light is then optically transferred to the actual detector.
  • Spectral information is typically lost.
  • The imaging resolution is limited due to the scattering of light inside the scintillator.
  • Predominant in X-ray imaging applications, especially in the field of X-ray microtomography.

Detector speed and resolution

X-ray detectors (typically) use scintillator screen to convert X-rays to visible light

Scintillator properties determine detection efficiency and image resolution (sharpness)

  • Thick scintillator: high efficiency, fast scanning, low resolution
  • Thin scintillator: low efficiency, only low energy X-ray detection, high resolution

Choosing a scintillator

Laboratory-based setups use magnification:

  • Pixel size not so important
  • Optimize energy response

Synchrotron-based setups use detector pixel size

  • Thin scintillators for high-quality high-resolution images: long scanning times (5-20 mins)
  • Thick scintillators for low-quality low-resolution images: fast scanning (down to less than 1 sec)
